Renderer & Shaders

MAME4droid can draw games with two different renderers. Choose between them under Options->Settings->Renderer & Shaders->Native Renderer (Shaders and HDR).

The two renderers

  • Legacy (Software) Renderer — the default: the older, extremely well-tested rendering path. It has no shader support and no HDR, but it's the safest, most compatible choice on every device, including low-end or older hardware. Stick with this one if you just want things to work with minimum fuss, or if your device struggles with the Native Renderer.
  • Native Renderer (Shaders and HDR): MAME's own modern rendering pipeline. Turning it on unlocks shader effects, True HDR for vector games, Auto HDR for regular (raster) games, and a much higher quality look for vector games in general (see below). It needs more GPU power than the legacy renderer, but on any reasonably modern device it's also faster when you're using high-resolution artwork/bezels, since its pipeline was specifically optimized for that case.

Important: once you enable the Native Renderer, also set Options->Settings->Emulation->Emulated Resolution to Native. This lets high-resolution artwork/bezels render at full quality with no performance penalty — leaving it at a lower fixed resolution while using the Native Renderer wastes the extra image quality you just unlocked.

Shaders

With the Native Renderer enabled, Renderer & Shaders->Effect shader lets you pick a post-processing effect (scanlines/CRT-style filters and similar), applied on top of the emulated picture. The available list depends on your device/build; select none to disable it. Shaders only affect the actual game screen — MAME's own menus and on-screen display stay crisp and unfiltered, so changing this never makes MAME's UI harder to read.

Vector games: advanced CRT simulation

Vector games (Asteroids, Tempest, Star Wars, Battlezone, etc. draw the picture with lines instead of a pixel grid) benefit the most from the Native Renderer. With Vector Advanced Effects enabled, MAME4droid runs a physically-based simulation of how a real vector CRT monitor actually drew those lines, instead of just drawing flat anti-aliased lines:

  • Optical bloom: light scattering/halos inside the tube glass around bright vectors.
  • Phosphor persistence (trails): the fading afterglow left behind by the electron beam, like a real phosphor screen.
  • Electron beam dynamics: short strokes (text, small ship details) burn brighter, exactly like the real hardware.
  • Corner burn: bright "weld points" where the beam decelerates on sharp corners.
  • Magnetic jitter/flicker: subtle coil noise and voltage fluctuation, for an authentic imperfect-CRT look instead of a too-perfect vector image.

Every one of these has its own calibration sliders under Vector Effects Calibration (Expert Mode) if you want to fine-tune the look; Restore CRT Defaults resets them all if you get lost. FBO Half Resolution renders the vector glow passes at half resolution to save GPU/battery, at the cost of slightly less crisp core lines — a good trade on less powerful devices.

HDR

If your device and display support HDR, HDR Settings->Enable HDR Display unlocks peak panel brightness and a wider color gamut:

  • Vector games (True HDR): phosphor brightness is rendered with real HDR luminance instead of being clamped to standard-dynamic-range white, closer to how intense the original CRT phosphors actually looked. CRT Base/Max Luminance calibrate the baseline and peak brightness (in nits); Dim Vector Artworks darkens the bezel around the game to make that contrast pop even more.
  • Raster games (Auto HDR): regular (non-vector) games and their artwork are standard-dynamic-range content, so MAME4droid can optionally upgrade them toward HDR luminance using inverse tone mapping (Enable Auto HDR (Raster)), with Auto HDR Intensity controlling how aggressively highlights get expanded, and Raster Paper White Reference setting the baseline brightness for comfort.

Note: HDR quality varies a lot between devices/panels — results are tuned primarily around well-behaved implementations (e.g. Google Pixel) and may look different, better or worse, on other hardware. Restore HDR Defaults is there if your calibration goes wrong.
